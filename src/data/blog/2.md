Our primary database and its replica had stopped speaking to each other. Not in the technical sense - replication was nominally working. But the lag had become... anthropomorphic.

| Time | Replication Lag | Emotional State |
|------|-----------------|-----------------|
| 00:00 UTC | 120ms | Distant |
| 06:00 UTC | 45ms | Warming up |
| 12:00 UTC | 380ms | Having an argument |
| 18:00 UTC | 1.2s | Silent treatment |
| 23:00 UTC | 45ms | Reconciliation |

The pattern repeated daily with the precision of a relationship conflict scheduler.

## The Theory

I developed seventeen hypotheses:

1. The replica was sulking
2. Network equipment was experiencing mid-life crisis
3. Our queries contained subliminal emotional triggers
4. The WAL logs were actually love letters, and the database was deliberately slowing down to preserve the romance
5-17. (Unable to articulate without caffeine)

```
[root@db-primary]# SELECT * FROM pg_stat_replication;
slot_name  | client_addr | state     | sync_state
pg_standby  | 10.0.2.15   | streaming | async (but why?)
```

## Resolution

We fixed it by not fixing it. The lag eventually stabilized at a level that could only be described as "companionable distance."

This mechanism has the following guarantees:

- Eventually all parties agree (after 2-3 hours and significant caffeine consumption)
- Quorum is achieved by simply having the most opinionated person type longest
- Byzantine fault tolerance is implicit because we assume at least one of us is confused at all times

Raft requires state machines. We have state *experiences*. Different, better.

Paxos requires proving properties. We have Discord emojis (reactions count as formal verification in our production system).
